NEW ENGLAND'S DARK DAY
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 19, 1780
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
The sky over New England went dark by mid-morning. Candles burned at noon, roosters returned to roost, and many believed Judgment Day had arrived. Modern science traces it to massive wildfires in Ontario combined with thick fog — but the awe stuck with a generation.

Colonial New England village at midday, May 19, 1780. Unnaturally darkened sky in deep amber and ashen gray, sun obscured by smoke-laden haze. White clapboard meetinghouse with simple steeple at center. Two or three figures in 1780s colonial dress (tricorn hats, long coats, women in caps and aprons) stand looking up, carrying candle lanterns. Warm candlelight from saltbox house windows. Bare and leafing maple trees. Horse and wooden cart idle. Hudson River School painting style. Hushed, reverent. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.
	SOURCE NOTE
Library of Congress; National Weather Service historical records; University of Missouri tree-ring study (2008).

THE HOMESTEAD ACT
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 20, 1862
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
Lincoln signed the Homestead Act, opening 160 acres of public land to any citizen willing to settle and improve it for five years. Between 1863 and 1900, roughly 400,000 claims moved 80 million acres into private hands — and reshaped the American West, for better and worse.

Plow leaning against the cabin. Wide open prairie grass stretching to the horizon. Warm afternoon light, long shadows. Realistic period painting style, evocative of Winslow Homer. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.
	SOURCE NOTE
National Archives; Library of Congress; Homestead National Historical Park.

CLARA BARTON FOUNDS THE RED CROSS
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 21, 1881
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
After watching the Civil War chew through soldiers without organized relief, Clara Barton spent twelve years lobbying for an American chapter of the Red Cross. On May 21, 1881, she founded it in Washington. Today it runs on roughly 300,000 volunteers — the system she built by refusing to be told no.
	IMAGE DIRECTION (for AG to generate)

Period-accurate Victorian dress, dark fabric with white collar, hair pulled back. Oil lamp casting warm light on the desk papers. American flag visible on the wall behind her. Composed, determined expression. Realistic portrait painting style, evocative of Civil War era American portraiture. Warm sepia and amber tones. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.
	SOURCE NOTE
American Red Cross historical archives; Library of Congress Manuscript Division.

THE AMNESTY ACT
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 22, 1872
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
Seven years after Appomattox, President Grant signed the Amnesty Act, restoring full civil and political rights to nearly all former Confederates. The intent was to stitch the country back together. The reality was more complicated — but the act marked a deliberate national choice to choose reconciliation over permanent punishment.

Dignified, serious tone. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.

SOUTH CAROLINA RATIFIES THE CONSTITUTION
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 23, 1788
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
South Carolina became the eighth state to ratify the U.S. Constitution, leaving just one more needed to make it the law of the land. The vote came after weeks of hard debate in Charleston — proof that the document survived by argument, not consensus. New Hampshire would push it over the line a month later.

Natural daylight through tall windows. Realistic Federalist-era oil painting style, evocative of Howard Chandler Christy's Signing of the Constitution. Warm wood tones. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.

WHAT HATH GOD WROUGHT
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 24, 1844
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
Samuel Morse sat in the Capitol and tapped out a message in code. Forty miles away in Baltimore, his partner received it: "What hath God wrought." The telegraph turned distance into a non-issue and rewired how America did business, war, and news. The world got smaller that morning.

His hand on the telegraph key, intent expression. Coiled wires running off the table. A small group of officials and observers in 1840s dress watching from behind. Warm natural light through tall arched windows. Realistic 19th-century American oil painting style. Historic, momentous tone. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.

Library of Congress; Smithsonian National Museum of American History.

May 25, 2026 — MEMORIAL DAY
DECORATION DAY
	HISTORICAL DATE
May 30, 1868 (first observed); May 25, 2026 (Memorial Day)
	STORY COPY (as it appears on the card)
Three years after the Civil War ended, General John A. Logan called for a national day to decorate the graves of the Union dead. On the first Decoration Day, May 30, 1868, flowers were laid at Arlington for both Union and Confederate soldiers. The name became Memorial Day. The duty didn't change.
	IMAGE DIRECTION (for AG to generate)
Arlington National Cemetery on the first Decoration Day, May 30, 1868. Rows of simple white wooden grave markers under tall trees, spring grass between them. Women in 1860s mourning dress (long dark skirts, bonnets) and Civil War veterans in faded blue Union coats placing fresh flowers — peonies, lilacs, wild roses — on the graves. A small American flag at one of the headstones. Soft late-afternoon light filtering through the trees. Solemn, reverent. Realistic 19th-century American oil painting style. Muted greens, deep blues, soft whites. No modern elements. No text. 16:9.
	SOURCE NOTE
National Cemetery Administration; Library of Congress; Arlington National Cemetery historical records.
