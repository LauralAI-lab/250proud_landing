Era: 1776–1800 · The Founding Era
Headline: The Declaration Almost Wasn't Signed on July 4th
Fact: Most of the 56 men who signed the Declaration of Independence didn't actually sign it on July 4, 1776.
Congress voted for independence on July 2nd, approved the text on the 4th, but most signatures weren't added
until August 2, 1776. John Adams thought July 2nd would be the holiday Americans celebrated forever. He was
wrong by two days.
Little-Known Fact: Two signers — Richard Henry Lee and Thomas McKean — didn't sign until
1776 was nearly over.

Era: 1776–1800 · The Founding Era
Headline: George Washington Lost More Battles Than He Won
Fact: George Washington lost roughly twice as many battles as he won during the Revolutionary War. What he
was brilliant at was keeping an army together and alive — retreating when he had to, striking when the odds
shifted. His crossing of the Delaware River on Christmas night 1776 to surprise Hessian troops at Trenton is
considered one of the boldest military moves in American history.
Little-Known Fact: Washington's army was so desperate for boots that winter that soldiers
left bloody footprints in the snow at Valley Forge.
Illustration Prompt: A wooden rowboat crossing an icy river at night, soldiers huddled with oars, chunks of ice in

Era: 1800–1850 · The Young Republic
Headline: The U.S. Capitol Burned Down. Twice.
Fact: In August 1814, British troops marched into Washington D.C. and set fire to the U.S. Capitol, the White
House, and several other federal buildings. It remains the only time since the Revolutionary War that a foreign
power has captured and occupied the U.S. capital. A sudden thunderstorm — possibly a tornado — helped
extinguish the fires and forced the British to retreat.
Little-Known Fact: Dolley Madison had the frame of Washington's portrait broken apart to
get the canvas out faster before fleeing.
Illustration Prompt: The Capitol building with dramatic storm clouds above, flames at windows, rain beginning to
fall — wide establishing shot, coloring book line art

Era: 1800–1850 · The Young Republic
Headline: Lewis and Clark Brought a Dog on the Entire Expedition
Fact: When Meriwether Lewis and William Clark set out in 1804 to explore the Louisiana Purchase — roughly
8,000 miles round trip through unmapped wilderness — Lewis brought his Newfoundland dog, Seaman. The dog
survived the whole journey, helped hunt food, warned the camp of buffalo and bears, and was mentioned dozens
of times in the expedition journals.
Little-Known Fact: Lewis paid $20 for Seaman before the trip — equivalent to about $400
today, nearly a month's wages for a laborer.
Illustration Prompt: A large shaggy Newfoundland dog sitting proudly at the bow of a wooden canoe, forested

Era: 1800–1850 · The Young Republic
Headline: Sequoyah Created an Entire Alphabet — By Himself
Fact: Sequoyah, a Cherokee silversmith who could not read or write in any language, spent over a decade
developing a writing system for the Cherokee language — entirely on his own. Completed around 1821, the
Cherokee syllabary had 86 characters. Within a few years, thousands of Cherokee people had learned to read
and write using it.
Little-Known Fact: The giant sequoia trees of California are named after him — the largest
living things on Earth carry his name.
Illustration Prompt: A man seated at a wooden table carving symbols into clay or scratching them onto bark,

Era: 1850–1865 · A Nation Divided
Headline: The Underground Railroad Ran on Courage and Code Words
Fact: The Underground Railroad wasn't a railroad at all — it was a secret network of people, safe houses, and
routes that helped enslaved people escape to free states and Canada. Harriet Tubman made 13 missions into the
South and guided approximately 70 people to freedom. She used the North Star for navigation and carried a gun
— both for protection and to ensure no one could turn back and endanger the others.
Little-Known Fact: Tubman never lost a single person she guided to freedom. She called
herself 'the conductor.'
Illustration Prompt: A person standing in a forest at night, looking up at a bright North Star through tree

Era: 1850–1865 · A Nation Divided
Headline: A Self-Taught Lawyer from Illinois Changed Everything
Fact: Abraham Lincoln had less than 12 months of formal schooling in his entire life. He taught himself law by
borrowing books and reading by firelight. He failed in business twice and lost multiple elections before becoming
the 16th President. The Emancipation Proclamation, issued January 1, 1863, declared enslaved people in
Confederate states to be free — changing what the Civil War was ultimately about.
Little-Known Fact: Lincoln is the only U.S. president to hold a patent — a device to lift
boats over shallow waters, invented in 1849.
Illustration Prompt: A tall figure reading a book by firelight in a log cabin, boots near the hearth, a candle on a

Era: 1865–1900 · Reconstruction & the Gilded Age
Headline: Two Railroads Met in the Middle of Nowhere and Connected a Country
Fact: On May 10, 1869, the Central Pacific and Union Pacific railroads met at Promontory Summit, Utah,
completing the first transcontinental railroad. Workers drove a ceremonial golden spike to mark the moment. For
the first time, you could travel from New York to San Francisco in about a week instead of six months by wagon.
Little-Known Fact: About 10,000 Chinese immigrant workers built the western half — paid
less than white counterparts and given the most dangerous jobs.
Illustration Prompt: Two massive steam locomotives facing each other nose-to-nose, steam billowing, a crowd

Era: 1865–1900 · Reconstruction & the Gilded Age
Headline: Thomas Edison Didn't Invent the Light Bulb — He Made It Work
Fact: Edison didn't create the concept of the light bulb — other inventors had been working on it for decades.
What he did in 1879 was develop a version with a carbon filament that lasted long enough to be practical, then
build an entire electrical distribution system to power it. His Menlo Park lab was the world's first industrial research
facility.
Little-Known Fact: Edison tested over 6,000 different materials for the filament before
finding one that worked.
Illustration Prompt: A glowing light bulb with visible filament, surrounded by notebooks, wires, and scientific

Era: 1900–1930 · The American Century Begins
Headline: Two Brothers from Ohio Flew for 12 Seconds and Changed the World
Fact: On December 17, 1903, Orville Wright piloted a motorized airplane at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, for 12
seconds and 120 feet. Neither man had a college degree. Their bicycle shop in Dayton, Ohio funded the whole
project. Within 66 years of that first flight, humans landed on the Moon.
Little-Known Fact: The Wright Brothers brought five witnesses that day. Only two local
newspapers covered the story — and both got the facts wrong.

Era: 1900–1930 · The American Century Begins
Headline: Women Earned the Right to Vote After 72 Years of Fighting
Fact: The women's suffrage movement began formally at the Seneca Falls Convention in 1848. It took 72 years
before the 19th Amendment was ratified on August 18, 1920. The deciding vote in Tennessee was cast by
24-year-old Harry Burn, who changed his vote after receiving a note from his mother urging him to 'be a good boy'
and support suffrage.
Little-Known Fact: Some of the women who started the movement in 1848 did not live to vote.

Era: 1930–1945 · Depression & World War
Headline: The Tuskegee Airmen Never Lost a Bomber They Were Assigned to Escort
Fact: The Tuskegee Airmen were the first Black military aviators in the U.S. Armed Forces, trained at Tuskegee
Army Air Field beginning in 1941. They flew over 15,000 missions in World War II and earned more than 150
Distinguished Flying Crosses. They flew in the face of a country that still didn't let them sit at the same lunch
counter as the men they protected in the air.
Little-Known Fact: Their red-painted tail fins earned them the nickname 'Red Tails' — and
bomber crews specifically requested them as escorts.

Era: 1930–1945 · Depression & World War
Headline: Rosie the Riveter Was Based on a Real Woman
Fact: During World War II, over six million American women entered the workforce to fill jobs left by men serving
overseas. The most famous 'Rosie the Riveter' image was painted by Norman Rockwell for the Saturday Evening
Post in 1943. The woman most historians believe inspired the name was Rosie Will Monroe, an actual riveter at a
Michigan aircraft plant.
Little-Known Fact: After the war, most women were pushed out of those jobs when the men
returned — but the workforce was never quite the same again.

Era: 1945–1970 · Postwar America
Headline: The Interstate Highway System Was Built for War — and Changed Everything Else
Fact: President Eisenhower signed the Federal Aid Highway Act in 1956, creating 41,000 miles of
controlled-access highway. Eisenhower was partly inspired by the German Autobahn and by the military's
difficulty moving equipment across the U.S. during the war. The highways transformed how Americans lived,
worked, shopped, and vacationed — and made the American road trip possible.
Little-Known Fact: By law, one mile in every five on the interstate system must be straight
— so it can be used as an airstrip in a national emergency.

Era: 1945–1970 · Postwar America
Headline: America Put a Man on the Moon with Less Computing Power Than Your Phone
Fact: On July 20, 1969, Neil Armstrong became the first human being to walk on the Moon. The Apollo Guidance
Computer had 4 kilobytes of RAM and ran at 0.043 MHz — your smartphone is roughly 100,000 times more
powerful. The mission was guided largely by handwritten calculations from NASA mathematician Katherine
Johnson, whose work was so trusted that John Glenn refused to fly unless she personally verified the computer's
numbers.
Little-Known Fact: Armstrong had less than 30 seconds of fuel left when he landed. Mission
Control knew. He didn't tell them he was worried.

Era: 1965–2000 · The Modern Age
Headline: The Internet Started as a Military Experiment with Four Computers
Fact: ARPANET sent its first message on October 29, 1969. It was supposed to say 'LOGIN.' The system crashed
after 'L' and 'O.' The four computers connected in that first network were at UCLA, Stanford, UC Santa Barbara,
and the University of Utah. By 2026, over five billion people use the internet daily. The first message ever sent
over what became the internet was 'LO' — accidentally the most poetic beginning imaginable.
Little-Known Fact: The first item ever sold on eBay, in 1995, was a broken laser pointer.
The buyer knew it was broken. He collected broken laser pointers.

Headline: One Speech in Washington Changed the Civil Rights Movement Forever
Fact: On August 28, 1963, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. delivered his 'I Have a Dream' speech to over 250,000
people at the March on Washington. Near the end, gospel singer Mahalia Jackson called out from behind him:
'Tell them about the dream, Martin!' He set aside his prepared notes and spoke from memory.
Little-Known Fact: The Lincoln Memorial steps where he spoke were the same steps where
Marian Anderson — refused a concert hall because she was Black — had sung to 75,000 people
in 1939.

Era: 2000–2026 · The New Century
Headline: An Ordinary Man Saved Thousands of Lives with a Bullhorn
Fact: Rick Rescorla — head of security for Morgan Stanley at the World Trade Center — had spent years
preparing his 2,700 employees for exactly this kind of emergency. On 9/11, he led employees out of the South
Tower singing Cornish folk songs through a bullhorn to keep them calm. All but 13 of his 2,700 employees
survived. Rescorla went back in to find the missing. He did not come out.
Little-Known Fact: Rescorla was a decorated Vietnam veteran and British immigrant who
became a U.S. citizen. He called America 'the greatest nation on Earth' until his last day.

Era: 2000–2026 · The New Century
Headline: America Still Builds Things That Go to the Stars
Fact: NASA's James Webb Space Telescope, launched December 25, 2021, can see galaxies that formed just
300 million years after the Big Bang — looking back 13.5 billion years in time. It is 100 times more powerful than
Hubble. Its golden mirror, 21 feet wide, was too large to launch in one piece — it unfolds in space like a flower.
Little-Known Fact: The Webb telescope orbits the Sun about 1 million miles from Earth — too
far for astronauts to repair. It unfolded perfectly on the first try.

Little-Known Fact: The average age of the men who signed the Declaration of Independence
